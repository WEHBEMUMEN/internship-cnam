Stiffness Matrix
Assembly

From geometric mapping to physical equilibrium.

1. The Principle of Virtual Work

In structural mechanics, the equilibrium of a body is found by minimizing the total potential energy. For a 2D elastic patch, this leads to the **Stiffness Matrix** $K$, which relates control point displacements $\mathbf{d}$ to external forces $\mathbf{f}$:

$$K = \sum_e K_e = \sum_e \int_{\hat{\Omega}_e} B^T D B |J| \, d\hat{\Omega}$$
  • $B$ is the **Strain-Displacement** matrix (derivatives of basis functions).
  • $D$ is the **Constitutive** matrix (material properties).
  • $|J|$ is the **Jacobian Determinant** (area scaling factor).

2. The B-Matrix for NURBS

Unlike standard FEM, where shape functions are local to each triangle/quad, IGA uses the global NURBS basis $R_i(\xi, \eta)$. The B-matrix maps the $x$ and $y$ displacements of a control point to the local strain vector $[\epsilon_{xx}, \epsilon_{yy}, \gamma_{xy}]^T$:

$$B_i = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial R_i}{\partial x} & 0 \\ 0 & \frac{\partial R_i}{\partial y} \\ \frac{\partial R_i}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial R_i}{\partial x} \end{bmatrix}$$

The physical derivatives are calculated by inverting the Jacobian matrix $J$:

$$\begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial R_i}{\partial x} \\ \frac{\partial R_i}{\partial y} \end{bmatrix} = J^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial R_i}{\partial \xi} \\ \frac{\partial R_i}{\partial \eta} \end{bmatrix}$$

3. Gaussian Quadrature

Because the integral is computationally expensive and the basis functions are non-polynomial (rational), we use **Gauss-Legendre Quadrature**. For quadratic $p=q=2$ patches, we typically use a $3 \times 3$ grid of Gauss points per knot span to ensure accurate integration of the $B^T D B$ term.

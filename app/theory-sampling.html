METHODOLOGY

High-Resolution Sampling

In Isogeometric Analysis, the geometry is defined as a continuous mathematical function. However, current computer screens work with pixels and discrete lines. To visualize a mathematical "infinite" curve, we must choose a specific resolution for rendering.

The Microscope Analogy

Imagine a real-world silk thread. Looking from a distance, it appears perfectly smooth. As you look through a microscope, you start to see the individual fibers. In our engine, "Sampling" is how many times we look through that microscope along the length of the beam. If we look only 5 times, the thread looks like a jagged line. If we look 500 times, it looks like a single, unbreakable fluid curve.

1. The 0.002 Parametric Step

The engine evaluates the B-Spline position at regular intervals in the Parametric Space (denoted by the Greek letter $\xi$). We use a step size of 0.002.

// The core loop for 500 segments for (let xi = 0; xi <= 1.0; xi += 0.002) { const point = evaluateNurbs(xi, controlPoints); ctx.lineTo(point.x, point.y); }

By dividing the parameter range [0, 1] into exactly 500 segments, we ensure that every pixel on the screen is accounted for, even when the beam is stretched to its maximum physical limit.

Performance Balance

Evaluating 500 points 60 times per second is the "Sweet Spot." It provides sub-pixel accuracy without overloading the browser's CPU.

Visual Anti-Aliasing

At this resolution, the browser's renderer can apply smooth edges automatically, eliminating "pixel crawl" during movement.

2. Adaptive vs. Uniform Sampling

While our current engine uses Uniform Sampling (fixed 0.002 step), professional CAD software often uses Adaptive Sampling, which places more points where the curve is sharp and fewer where it is straight.

In our Digital Twin project, we chose fixed high-resolution sampling to ensure a predictable frame rate (Strict Real-time), which is critical for synchronized engineering sensors.

PHASE 1.3

Knot Insertion (h-Refinement)

In traditional engineering simulations, if an engineer needs more precision in a specific area (like measuring the stress on an airplane wing joint), they simply chop the mesh structure into thousands of smaller triangles. In Isogeometric Analysis, chopping up the mesh ruins the mathematical elegance. Instead, we use a profound mathematical trick called Knot Insertion.

The "Puppet String" Analogy

Imagine a beautifully posed marionette puppet hanging from the ceiling, controlled by four strings attached to its arms and legs.

Now, you decide you want finer control over one specific elbow joint. To get that control, you need to add a brand new string (a new Control Point) right in the middle of the arm.

However! If you blindly tie a new string and pull it, you'll yank the arm and mess up the beautiful pose you spent hours creating. Knot insertion is the mathematical algorithm that allows you to sneakily tie a new string onto the elbow, adjust its tension perfectly, and hold it without making the puppet flinch or move a single millimeter. You gain extreme control, natively, without breaking the pose.

1. Why Insert a Knot?

A "knot" ($\xi$) is a specific number that dictates where a "magnet" (Control Point) starts and stops pulling on the curve. By inserting a new knot into the curve's timeline, the math gives us a "free" Control Point to play with right at that exact location. We call this h-refinement because we are reducing step size ($h$, the distance between knots).

Preserving Geometries Exactly

Let's say we have an original curve defined by $n+1$ control points $\mathbf{P}_i$. We want to insert a new knot $\bar{\xi}$ between $\xi_k$ and $\xi_{k+1}$.

The goal is to find $n+2$ new control points $\mathbf{Q}_i$ such that the physical curve does not move at all:

$$\mathbf{C}(\xi) = \sum_{i=0}^{n} R_{i,p}(\xi) \mathbf{P}_i \equiv \sum_{i=0}^{n+1} \bar{R}_{i,p}(\xi) \mathbf{Q}_i$$

This means the left side of the equation (the old curve) exactly equals the right side (the new curve with an extra control point).

2. Boehm's Algorithm

Instead of relying on an approximation or a curve fit (like we did in Phase 1.2), Knot Insertion can actually be solved algebraically! The most famous and elegant way to solve it is using Boehm's Algorithm.

Boehm's Algorithm figures out exactly how to shift the existing strings (control points) and attach the new one so the puppet doesn't flinch. Because the new knot $\bar{\xi}$ only affects a small local area, we don't have to change all the control points—only a few neighbors!

1

Locate the Insertion Point

Find the knot span index $k$ where the new knot $\bar{\xi}$ belongs: $\xi_k \le \bar{\xi} < \xi_{k+1}$.

2

Keep Safe Points Safe

Any magnets (Control Points) that are far to the left or far to the right of the insertion point don't need to change. The computer simply copies them over directly: $\mathbf{Q}_i = \mathbf{P}_i$ (for the far left) and $\mathbf{Q}_i = \mathbf{P}_{i-1}$ (for the far right).

3

Calculate the Blended "Alpha" Factors

For the $p$ control points that sit right in the middle of the "danger zone", Boehm's algorithm calculates a blending factor $\alpha_i$ based on the new knot's position:

$$\alpha_i = \frac{\bar{\xi} - \xi_i}{\xi_{i+p} - \xi_i}$$
4

Blend the Neighbors

Finally, the computer calculates the new positions for these middle magnets by blending their old position with their neighbor's old position, perfectly shifting the tension.

$$\mathbf{Q}_i = \alpha_i \mathbf{P}_i + (1 - \alpha_i) \mathbf{P}_{i-1}$$

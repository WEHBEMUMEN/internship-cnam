PHASE 1.1

NURBS & Cox-de Boor Recursion

Non-Uniform Rational B-Splines (NURBS) are the industry standard for creating complex, perfectly smooth shapes in engineering and computer graphics. While the math may look intimidating, the core concept is actually incredibly intuitive once you break it down.

The "Magnetic Ruler" Analogy

Imagine holding a highly flexible metal ruler (the spline curve). To shape this ruler, you place powerful magnets around it. These magnets are what we call Control Points. As you move a magnet around, the metal ruler bends to follow it without ever snapping or creating a sharp crease.

Now, what if some magnets are stronger than others? A stronger magnet pulls the ruler much closer to itself. In NURBS, this magnetic strength is called the Weight. If the weight is extremely high, the curve will snap tightly to that specific control point.

1. The Mathematical Foundation

A computer doesn't naturally know how to draw a smooth curve. Instead, it runs an algorithm to figure out: "For any microscopic piece of this curve, which magnets are pulling on it, and exactly how hard?"

This "pull factor" is determined by the B-spline basis function. It assigns an area of influence to every control point using a list of numbers called a knot vector ($\Xi$). A knot vector acts like a timeline, telling the algorithm exactly where a control point's magnetic field begins, peaks, and fades to zero.

The Cox-de Boor Algorithm

This beautiful recursive formula calculates the exact influence of each control point at a given fraction along the curve ($\xi$).

For a degree of zero (hard, flat steps):

$$N_{i,0}(\xi) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } \xi_i \le \xi < \xi_{i+1} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

For a smoother, higher degree $p$ (where influence fades in and out smoothly):

$$N_{i,p}(\xi) = \frac{\xi - \xi_i}{\xi_{i+p} - \xi_i} N_{i,p-1}(\xi) + \frac{\xi_{i+p+1} - \xi}{\xi_{i+p+1} - \xi_{i+1}} N_{i+1,p-1}(\xi)$$

2. Making it "Rational"

The "R" in NURBS stands for Rational. In mathematics, rational simply means involving fractions or division. We introduce division to account for the varying strengths (weights, $w_i$) of our magnets.

If we just multiplied the basis functions by the weights, the whole curve would just scale up un-evenly. By dividing by the sum of all weights, we ensure the math stays balanced, effectively "normalizing" the magnetic field.

$$R_{i,p}(\xi) = \frac{N_{i,p}(\xi) w_i}{W(\xi)}, \quad \text{where the total pull is } W(\xi) = \sum_{j=1}^{n} N_{j,p}(\xi) w_j$$

3. Drawing the Curve

Once the computer knows exactly how hard every magnet is pulling at a specific percentage along the curve, drawing it is straight-forward. It multiplies the influence $R(\xi)$ by the coordinates of the Control Points $P$.

1

Locate the Zone

Find the knot span index $k$. This tells us which segment of the "timeline" we are in, and lets the computer completely ignore any magnets that are too far away to have an effect.

2

Compute Influence

Use the recursive formula to compute how strongly each active control point is pulling: $N_{i,p}(\xi)$.

3

Apply Weights (Rationalize)

Multiply each influence by its magnet's custom weight, and divide by the total sum to get the final adjusted influence: $R_{i,p}(\xi)$.

4

Assemble the Coordinates

The final physical position of the curve is just the sum of the control point positions scaled by their influence: $\mathbf{C}(\xi) = \sum R_{i,p}(\xi) \mathbf{P}_i$. This is done for the X, Y, and Z axes simultaneously!

Executive Summary

The jump from 1D curves to 2D surfaces is the cornerstone of structural isogeometric analysis (IGA). While 1D beams model axial and bending tension along a line, 2D surfaces represent Plates, Shells, and Membranes. This phase establishes the mathematical mapping between the parametric unit square $[0,1]^2$ and physical 3D space.

1. Tensor Product Basis

To define a surface, we don't just "add" a dimension. We perform a Tensor Product of two orthogonal univariate basis systems. Given two knot vectors $\Xi = \{ \xi_0, \xi_1, ... \xi_{n+p+1} \}$ and $\mathcal{H} = \{ \eta_0, \eta_1, ... \eta_{m+q+1} \}$, the bivariate basis functions are:

$$R_{i,j}^{p,q}(\xi, \eta) = \frac{N_{i,p}(\xi) M_{j,q}(\eta) w_{i,j}}{\sum_{k=0}^{n} \sum_{l=0}^{m} N_{k,p}(\xi) M_{l,q}(\eta) w_{k,l}}$$

This construction ensures that:

  • Local Support: $R_{i,j}$ is non-zero only on the rectangle $[\xi_i, \xi_{i+p+1}] \times [\eta_j, \eta_{j+q+1}]$.
  • Partition of Unity: $\sum \sum R_{i,j}(\xi, \eta) = 1$ for all parametric coordinates, ensuring rigid-body motion preservation.
  • Product Continuity: The surface inherits the continuity of its components ($C^{p-k}$ and $C^{q-l}$).

2. Surface Mapping & Tangents

A NURBS surface $\mathbf{S}(\xi, \eta)$ maps the parametric unit square to 3D space via the control point grid $\mathbf{P}_{i,j}$. To perform mechanics (calculating strain/stress), we must find the Tangent Vectors at any point:

$$\mathbf{a}_1 = \frac{\partial \mathbf{S}}{\partial \xi}, \quad \mathbf{a}_2 = \frac{\partial \mathbf{S}}{\partial \eta}$$

These vectors define the local coordinate system on the surface. The Surface Normal $\mathbf{n}$ is then calculated via the cross-product, which is essential for modeling pressure loads on membranes:

$$\mathbf{n} = \frac{\mathbf{a}_1 \times \mathbf{a}_2}{\| \mathbf{a}_1 \times \mathbf{a}_2 \|}$$

3. The Jacobian & Integration

In Finite Element Analysis, we integrate stiffness over the physical volume. Since our equations are solved in the parametric space $(\xi, \eta)$, we must transform the differential area $dA$ using the **Jacobian Matrix** $\mathbf{J}$:

$$\mathbf{J} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial \xi} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial \eta} \\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial \xi} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial \eta} \\ \frac{\partial z}{\partial \xi} & \frac{\partial z}{\partial \eta} \end{bmatrix}$$

The magnitude of the transformation is given by the determinant-like quantity (Area Factor):

$$dA = \| \mathbf{a}_1 \times \mathbf{a}_2 \| \, d\xi d\eta$$

Conservation of Physical Area

If moving a control point pushes the physical surface apart (dilation), the Jacobian increases, signifying that more "mass" or "stiffness" is represented by that parametric region. This ensures that physical laws like Conservation of Mass are respected regardless of how distorted the NURBS grid becomes.

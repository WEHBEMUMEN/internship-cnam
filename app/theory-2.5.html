Phase 2.5 | Calculus

Metric Mapping:
The Jacobian

Transforming parametric space into physical area.

1. The Mapping Calculus

In Isogeometric Analysis, we perform numerical integration in the fixed parametric space $\hat{\Omega} = [0,1]^2$. To calculate physical quantities like energy or stiffness, we must relate a small "differential" area $d\xi d\eta$ in parametric space to its corresponding area $dA$ in physical space.

This transformation is governed by the Jacobian Matrix $J$, which consists of the tangent vectors of the surface:

$$J = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial \xi} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial \eta} \\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial \xi} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial \eta} \\ \frac{\partial z}{\partial \xi} & \frac{\partial z}{\partial \eta} \end{bmatrix} = [ \mathbf{S}_\xi, \mathbf{S}_\eta ]$$

The **Jacobian Determinant** $|J|$ (specifically the magnitude of the cross product for surfaces) represents the area scaling factor:

$$|J| = \| \mathbf{S}_\xi \times \mathbf{S}_\eta \|$$ $$dA = |J| \, d\xi \, d\eta$$

2. Analytical Rational Derivatives

Because NURBS are rational functions ($\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{A}/W$), we must use the quotient rule to find exact derivatives. For a numerator vector $\mathbf{A}$ and weight denominator $W$:

$$\mathbf{S}_\xi = \frac{\mathbf{A}_\xi W - \mathbf{A} W_\xi}{W^2} = \frac{\mathbf{A}_\xi - \mathbf{S} W_\xi}{W}$$

This analytical approach allows for perfect precision during integration, avoiding the errors inherent in finite-difference approximations.

3. Why it matters for Analysis

The Jacobian is not just for visualization; it is the core of the Isoparametric Concept. By knowing $J$, we can compute the gradient of shape functions in physical space, which is required to assemble the stiffness matrix for structural simulations.

PHASE 1.4

Vector Calculus & Tangents

In Isogeometric Analysis, drawing a beautiful curve is only half the battle. To actually run physics simulations—like calculating how an airplane wing bends in the wind or how heat travels through a metal pipe—we need to know exactly how the curve is behaving locally. We need its derivatives.

The "Headlight Beam" Analogy

Imagine you are driving a car along a curvy racetrack in pitch darkness. The mathematical curve $\mathbf{C}(\xi)$ tells you exactly where the car is on the track.

Now, imagine turning on your headlights. The straight beam of light piercing through the darkness represents the Tangent Vector (the first derivative, $C'(\xi)$). If you suddenly let go of the steering wheel, the car would fly off the track exactly in the direction of the headlights.

In engineering, knowing exactly where the headlights are pointing at every single millimeter of the track is critical. It tells the computer the exact orientation and slope of the material, allowing it to calculate stresses, strains, and geometric normals.

1. The Problem with Approximations

Many basic software programs calculate tangents by looking at two points very close together on the curve and drawing a straight line between them. This is called a numerical approximation. While this works for video games, it is disastrous for engineering analysis because those tiny errors snowball into massive miscalculations when predicting structural failure.

Analytical Brilliance

Because NURBS are true polynomials, we can use pure calculus to find the exact, perfect formula for the tangent, with zero approximation error. This is called an analytical derivative.

$$\mathbf{C}'(\xi) = \frac{d}{d\xi} \sum_{i=0}^{n} R_{i,p}(\xi) \mathbf{P}_i = \sum_{i=0}^{n} R'_{i,p}(\xi) \mathbf{P}_i$$

To find the tangent of the curve, we just need to find the derivative of the influence fields ($R'_{i,p}$) multiplied by the control point positions!

2. Differentiating Rational Functions

Since the basis function $R_{i,p}$ is a fraction (remember the $W(\xi)$ division from Phase 1.1?), we must use the standard Quotient Rule from calculus to split it open:

The Quotient Rule

The derivative of a fraction $\frac{A}{B}$ is $\frac{A'B - AB'}{B^2}$. For our NURBS basis functions, this expands perfectly into:

$$R'_{i,p}(\xi) = \frac{A'_i(\xi) W(\xi) - A_i(\xi) W'(\xi)}{(W(\xi))^2}$$

Where $A_i(\xi) = N_{i,p}(\xi) w_i$.

3. The Engine Calculation Steps

To render the tangent vector on your screen, the 1.4 Phase Engine executes these specific steps at 60 FPS:

1

Base Derivatives

First, it calculates the derivative of the raw (non-rational) B-spline function $N'_{i,p}(\xi)$ using a modified Cox-de Boor recursion that drops the degree by one: $p \to p-1$.

2

Weight Derivatives

It computes how the overall magnetic field (the total weight $W$) is changing: $W'(\xi) = \sum N'_{j,p}(\xi) w_j$.

3

Quotient Assembly

It applies the Quotient Rule to calculate the exact, true derivative $R'_{i,p}(\xi)$.

4

Vector Combination

Finally, it multiplies these new $R'$ values by the Control Point coordinates to get the definitive $(X, Y)$ vector of the tangent, rendering the "headlight beam" perfectly onto the canvas.

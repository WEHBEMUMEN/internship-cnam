Exact Circle Representation

A circle cannot be represented exactly by polynomial B-splines. However, NURBS (Non-Uniform Rational B-Splines) use rational weights $w_i$ to project a polynomial curve from a higher-dimensional space, enabling exact representation of all conic sections — circles, ellipses, parabolas.

$$\mathbf{C}(\xi) = \frac{\sum_{i=0}^{n} N_{i,p}(\xi) \, w_i \, \mathbf{P}_i}{\sum_{j=0}^{n} N_{j,p}(\xi) \, w_j}$$

The Phase 1.8 engine constructs a unit circle using 9 quadratic ($p=2$) control points arranged symmetrically. The key insight is the weight pattern:

Weight Configuration

Corner control points (on the circle) carry $w = 1.0$, while midpoint control points (off the circle, pulling it outward) carry $w = \cos(\pi/4) \approx 0.7071$. This specific ratio produces an exact circular arc between each pair of corner points.

$$w_{corner} = 1.0, \quad w_{mid} = \cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}$$

Unlike a polygon approximation that visually looks circular at high resolution, this NURBS circle is mathematically exact — every single evaluated point lies precisely on the unit circle to machine precision.

p-Refinement (Degree Elevation)

Degree elevation increases the polynomial degree $p$ of the basis functions to $p + 1$ while preserving the exact same geometric curve. The number of control points increases, and the knot vector is enriched with repeated knots.

$$p \to p + 1, \quad n_{cp} \uparrow, \quad \text{Geometry unchanged}$$

How It Works

The engine uses a Least Squares projection approach: after building the new knot vector (with elevated degree and appropriately increased knot multiplicities), the new control points $\mathbf{P}'_i$ are computed by sampling the original curve densely and solving a fitting system against the new, higher-degree basis functions.

Why It Matters for IGA

Higher-degree bases provide superior approximation for stress/strain fields in structural analysis. A cubic ($p=3$) basis has $C^2$ continuity at knot spans, giving smooth second derivatives — critical for accurate bending moment calculations.

h-Refinement (Knot Insertion)

Knot insertion adds new knot values into the knot vector, splitting existing elements into smaller ones. This increases the number of control points and elements without changing the degree or the geometry.

$$\bar{\xi} \in [u_k, u_{k+1}) \implies \mathbf{P}'_i = (1 - \alpha_i)\mathbf{P}_{i-1} + \alpha_i \mathbf{P}_i$$

where the blending coefficients $\alpha_i$ are computed from the local knot ratios:

$$\alpha_i = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } u_{i+p} \geq \bar{\xi} \\ \frac{\bar{\xi} - u_i}{u_{i+p} - u_i} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Selective Density

Unlike global mesh refinement in classical FEM, h-refinement allows engineers to pack denser control points locally around areas of high stress concentration while keeping the rest of the domain coarse. The Phase 1.8 engine inserts midpoint knots into every unique span uniformly.

k-Refinement (Order Elevation + Knot Insertion)

k-Refinement is the unique IGA-native refinement strategy that has no analogue in classical FEM. It combines degree elevation (p-refine) first, then performs knot insertion (h-refine) second. The order matters critically.

Strategy Order of Operations Resulting Continuity
h then p Insert knots first, then elevate degree $C^0$ at new knots (FEM-like)
k-Refinement (p then h) Elevate degree first, then insert knots $C^{p-1}$ at new knots (Hyper-smooth)

The Key Advantage

When you elevate degree before inserting knots, the newly inserted knots inherit the maximum continuity of the elevated space ($C^{p_{new}-1}$). If you reverse the order (h then p), the inserted knots are already locked at $C^0$ and degree elevation cannot recover the lost smoothness. This is why k-refinement achieves dramatically better accuracy per degree of freedom in IGA simulations.

Continuity: IGA vs Classical FEM

The fundamental advantage of Isogeometric Analysis over classical Finite Element meshes lies in inter-element continuity:

Property Classical FEM IGA (NURBS)
Inter-element continuity $C^0$ (position only) $C^{p-k}$ (derivatives smooth)
Basis function support 1 element $p+1$ elements
Exact geometry Approximated (faceted) Exact (same as CAD)
Stress field quality Discontinuous between elements Smooth across entire domain

In Phase 1.8, you can observe this directly: applying k-refinement to the circle increases the number of elements and control points while maintaining $C^{p-1}$ smoothness everywhere — the basis function plot shows broad, overlapping, smooth curves rather than the narrow, disconnected spikes of classical FEM shape functions.

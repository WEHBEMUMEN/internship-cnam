UNDER THE HOOD

The Javascript V8 Engine

Calculating the complex Cox-de Boor recursions for hundreds of segments is mathematically demanding. Doing it interactively, in a standard web browser without crashing the user's computer, requires specific computer science techniques.

The "Flipbook vs Engine" Analogy

Most 3D animations you watch online (like a Pixar movie or a pre-rendered engineering video) are Flipbooks. A supercomputer spent weeks calculating the physics and drawing each frame. When you click play, it simply flips through those static pictures quickly. You cannot touch or alter a flipbook while it's playing.

The Simulation Hub is a true Rendering Engine. It does not load pre-drawn pictures. Instead, every 16 milliseconds (60 times a second), the JavaScript V8 engine recalculates the entire mathematical state of the universe from scratch and draws the curve onto an HTML5 Canvas. Because it rebuilds the universe every frame, you can reach in (via your mouse) and grab a control point, and the system instantly incorporates your actions into the next frame's physics.

1. Efficient Recursion

The Cox-de Boor algorithm is a "recursive" function—meaning it calls itself over and over to solve a problem. Poorly written recursive algorithms cause computers to freeze as they get stuck in infinite loops or run out of memory.

Our JS engine utilizes an optimized looping structure that calculates lower-degree basis functions first and securely holds them in local variables. It then continuously builds the higher degrees on top of those cached values. This reduces a potentially exponential $O(2^p)$ nightmare into an incredibly fast, linear $O(p^2)$ operation.

2. The Animation Request Loop

To draw smoothly, the engine locks into the browser's native `requestAnimationFrame` API.

1

Wipe the Slate

The entire `<canvas>` context is completely erased. The screen becomes instantly blank.

2

Evaluate State

The engine checks the `mouseX` and `mouseY` variables. If the user is dragging a control point, it updates the math matrix with the new coordinates immediately.

3

Matrix Mapping (0.002 Sampling)

The mathematical Cox-de Boor evaluation fires, calculating exactly 500 positional segments for the curve across the updated knot spans.

4

Vector Rendering

The HTML5 Canvas API takes those 500 coordinates and traces a continuous pixel line `ctx.lineTo(x, y)` between them, then fills it with vibrant gradients. The entire process takes less than 2 milliseconds, well under the 16ms budget required for 60 FPS!

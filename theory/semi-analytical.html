1. The Analytical Baseline

To verify the Phase 3.3 IGA Solver, we compare its response at low load ranges against the exact mathematical solution for the torsion of a 2D elastic annulus. In infinitesimal strain theory, the radial displacement $u_r = 0$, and the tangential displacement $u_\theta(r)$ is derived directly from the axisymmetric equilibrium equations.

Lamé's Annulus Torsion Solution

$$ u_\theta(r) = \frac{T_{ext}}{4\pi G} \left( \frac{r}{R_i^2} - \frac{1}{r} \right) $$

Where $T_{ext}$ is the applied torque, $G = \frac{E}{2(1+\nu)}$ is the shear modulus, and $R_i, R_o$ are the inner and outer radii.

2. The Nonlinear Limitation

Why do we use the linear analytical solution instead of a 1D nonlinear formulation? Because Follower Forces are Non-Conservative.

If we try to write a 1D potential energy $\Pi(u, \phi)$ for the nonlinear ring, we must assume a "dead load" (constant direction) to take the derivative. However, the follower torque in Phase 3.3 rotates with the material. This breaks the existence of a scalar potential field, meaning a 1D energy-minimization solver will mathematically diverge or produce `NaN` at high rotations.

This is exactly why the 2D IGA solver (Phase 3.3) is required: It builds the tangent stiffness matrix directly from the weak form, allowing it to integrate non-conservative follower forces accurately where simple analytical models fail.

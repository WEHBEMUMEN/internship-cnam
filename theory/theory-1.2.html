PHASE 1.2

Degree Elevation & Fitting

Degree elevation (also known as p-refinement) is one of the superpowers of Isogeometric Analysis. It allows us to increase the complexity and smoothness of our mathematical model without changing the actual physical shape of the object we are studying.

The "Flexible Wire" Analogy

Imagine you have a piece of stiff wire (a low-degree curve, like $p=1$ or $2$). You bend it to create a specific shape. Now, you realize you'll need to make more intricate bends later, but your stiff wire won't allow for those tight, microscopic details.

Degree elevation is like magically swapping that stiff wire for a much more flexible one (a higher-degree curve). But here is the trick: before you swap them, you pin the new flexible wire down so it perfectly traces the exact same shape as the old stiff wire. Now you have the exact same shape, but with infinitely more potential to be manipulated delicately later on.

1. The Goal of p-Refinement

In standard Finite Element Analysis (FEA), making a model more precise usually means chopping it up into thousands of tiny linear triangles—making a jagged, $C^0$ continuous mesh. This "loss of smoothness" can corrupt engineering calculations like stress and heat transfer.

In IGA, we can make a model more precise by increasing the polynomial degree $p$ of the underlying basis functions. A higher $p$ creates smoother, more accurate physics equations ($C^{p-1}$ continuity) across the entire geometry, without chopping anything up.

Preserving the Geometry

If we have an original curve $\mathbf{C}(\xi)$ of degree $p$, we want to find a new set of control points $\mathbf{P}'_i$ for a degree $p+1$ curve such that:

$$\sum_{i=0}^{n} R_{i,p}(\xi) \mathbf{P}_i = \sum_{i=0}^{n'} R_{i,p+1}(\xi) \mathbf{P}'_i$$

For every possible value of $\xi$. This is generally impossible to do purely geometrically without an advanced algorithm, because elevating the degree fundamentally changes how every "magnet" (Control Point) interacts with the curve.

2. The Solution: Curve Fitting

Instead of trying to solve the impossibly complex algebraic knot equations directly, our 1.2 Engine uses an incredibly clever workaround: Least Squares Curve Fitting.

1

Sample the Original Curve

The computer takes the original, stiff wire (degree $p$) and places hundreds of invisible "tracking dots" ($\mathbf{S}_k$) along its length. It memorizes the exact $X$ and $Y$ coordinates of every dot.

2

Generate the Flexible Wire

Next, it generates a completely new knot vector and a fresh set of basis functions for degree $p+1$. It doesn't know where the control points should go yet, but it has the formula ready.

3

The Least Squares Optimization

The algorithm uses a mathematical technique called Least Squares to find the perfect positions for the new magnets ($\mathbf{P}'_i$). It moves the new control points around computationally until the new flexible wire minimizes the distance to every single tracking dot simultaneously.

The Error Minimization

The goal is to find the $\mathbf{P}'_i$ that makes the sum of squared errors as close to zero as theoretically possible:

$$E = \sum_{k=0}^{M} \left( \sum_{i=0}^{n'} R_{i, p+1}(\xi_k) \mathbf{P}'_i - \mathbf{S}_k \right)^2$$

Because the new curve is mathematically superior to the old curve, the projection is essentially exact. Visually, the curve doesn't move a single pixel, but under the hood, it's now a high-fidelity $p+1$ polynomial!

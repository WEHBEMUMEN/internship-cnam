Euler-Bernoulli Theory & Derivation

The beam behavior is governed by local equations relating moment $M$, shear $V$, and load $q$. In the Phase 1.7 engine, we derive the Weak Form using the principle of virtual work.

Equilibrium: $M_{,xx} + q = 0$
Constitutive: $M = -EI w_{,xx}$

To derive the weak form, we multiply by a virtual displacement $\delta w$ and integrate. Through Integration by Parts (IPP), derivatives are transferred:

$$\int_0^L (M_{,xx} + q) \delta w \, dx = 0$$ $$\text{IPP1: } -\int_0^L M_{,x} \delta w_{,x} \, dx + [M_{,x} \delta w]_0^L + \int_0^L q \delta w \, dx = 0$$ $$\text{IPP2: } \int_0^L M \delta w_{,xx} \, dx - [M \delta w_{,x}]_0^L + \int_0^L q \delta w \, dx = 0$$

With appropriate boundary conditions, the final weak form for flexural bending is: $$\int_0^L EI w_{,xx} \delta w_{,xx} \, dx = \int_0^L q(x) \delta w \, dx$$

Mathematics: Knot Vectors & Basis Functions

In the Phase 1.7 engine, the geometry is defined using Non-Uniform Rational B-Splines (NURBS). The domain is dictated by a Knot Vector ($\Xi$), which divides the parametric space into elements.

Example Knot Vector: $\Xi = \{0, 0, 0, \xi_a, \xi_b, \xi_c, 1, 1, 1\}$

The engine uses an Open Uniform Knot Vector. This means the ends are clamped with $p+1$ repeating values, forcing interpolation at the boundaries. The basis functions are computed via the Cox-de Boor formula:

$$N_{i,0}(\xi) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } u_i \le \xi < u_{i+1} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$ $$N_{i,p}(\xi)=\frac{\xi - u_i}{u_{i+p} - u_i} N_{i,p-1}(\xi) + \frac{u_{i+p+1} - \xi}{u_{i+p+1} - u_{i+1}} N_{i+1,p-1}(\xi)$$

For precise geometric circularity, we use weights $w_i$: $R_{i,p}(\xi) = \frac{N_{i,p}(\xi) w_i}{\sum N_{j,p}(\xi) w_j}$.

Isoparametric Transformation & Jacobian

We map the physical domain $x$ to a parametric domain $\xi \in [0, 1]$. Because the Euler-Bernoulli weak form requires second derivatives, the Jacobian ($J = x^h_{,\xi}$) plays a critical role.

Chain Rule: $w_{,x} = \frac{1}{J} w^h_{,\xi} \quad ; \quad w_{,xx} = \frac{1}{J^2} w^h_{,\xi\xi}$

The resulting stiffness matrix $\mathbf{K}$ requires integration with the Jacobian to the third power:

$$K_{ij} = \int_0^1 EI \left( \frac{1}{J^2} B_i \right) \left( \frac{1}{J^2} B_j \right) J \, d\xi = \int_0^1 EI \frac{B_i B_j}{J^3} \, d\xi$$

The C¹ Necessity

If degree $p=1$ (Linear), then $w^h_{,\xi\xi} = 0$. Flexural bending requires $p \ge 2$ to maintain non-zero curvature within the elements.

Reduced Order Modeling (ROM) & SVD

For real-time Digital Twin synchronization, the engine uses Galerkin Projection to compress a high-fidelity model ($N$ DoF) into a microscopic modal subspace ($m \ll N$). This projection allows the browser to solve complex mechanics in microseconds by bypassing the $O(n^3)$ cost of full matrix inversion.

The Fundamental Shift:
Instead of solving $\mathbf{K} \mathbf{u} = \mathbf{F}$, we approximate the solution as a linear combination of Dominant Modes stored in a basis matrix $\mathbf{V}$: $$\mathbf{u} \approx \mathbf{V} \mathbf{q}$$ $$\mathbf{V}^T \mathbf{K} \mathbf{V} \mathbf{q} = \mathbf{V}^T \mathbf{F} \quad \to \quad \mathbf{K}_{red} \mathbf{q} = \mathbf{F}_{red}$$ Where $\mathbf{K}_{red}$ is a tiny $m \times m$ matrix, solvable instantaneously at 60 FPS.

Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD)

The "Learning" phase involves collecting high-fidelity snapshots $\mathbf{S} = [\mathbf{u}_1, \dots, \mathbf{u}_k]$ across different load parameters. We then use Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to extract the most energetic modes:

$$\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{U} \mathbf{\Sigma} \mathbf{W}^T$$

Each column of $\mathbf{U}$ represents a characteristic shape of the bridge. The diagonal values in $\mathbf{\Sigma}$ ($\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \dots$) represent the "importance" or energy of each shape.

Singular Value Truncation & Energy ($\eta$)

We decide how many modes ($m$) to keep by evaluating the Cumulative Energy Retention ($\eta$):

$$\eta(m) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^m \sigma_i}{\sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i} \geq 0.9999$$

Mathematically, SVD is equivalent to find the eigenvectors of the Covariance Matrix $\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{S} \mathbf{S}^T$. By keeping only the modes above $\eta=99.99\%$, we ensure the reduced model is physically identical to the full model within floating-point tolerance.

Latent Space Projection

In the Online phase, the physics equations are projected into the Latent Space of coefficients $\mathbf{q}$. The high-fidelity state $\mathbf{u}$ is never actually solved; we only update the tiny state vector $\mathbf{q}$ and reconstruct the visual mesh:

$\mathbf{q}_{t+1} = \mathbf{K}_{red}^{-1} \mathbf{F}_{red}$
$\mathbf{u}_{mesh} = \mathbf{V} \cdot \mathbf{q}_{t+1}$

Modal Energy & Mode Selection (RIC)

To determine the optimal value for $m$, we evaluate the Relative Information Content (RIC). This represents the fraction of the total "energy" (variance) captured by the first $m$ modes:

$$\text{RIC}(m) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^m \sigma_i^2}{\sum_{i=1}^p \sigma_i^2} > \text{Tol}$$

In our Phase 1.7 engine, the bridge simulation typically achieves $\text{RIC} > 0.9999$ with only **3 to 5 modes**, even for high-resolution 100-node IGA models.

Basis Orthonormality & Stability

A critical property of the POD basis $\mathbf{V}$ derived via SVD is its orthonormality:

$$\mathbf{V}^T \mathbf{V} = \mathbf{I}_{m \times m}$$

This property ensures that the projection does not amplify noise and that the reduced stiffness matrix $\mathbf{K}_{red}$ is well-conditioned, avoiding numerical instabilities when solving in the latent space.

Offline vs. Online Workflow

The Phase 1.7 engine splits the computation into two distinct workflows:

  • 1. Offline Phase (The Brain):
    Compute full IGA stiffness $\mathbf{K}$, generate snapshots for $\mu \in [0, L]$, perform SVD, pre-calculate $\mathbf{K}_{red} = \mathbf{V}^T \mathbf{K} \mathbf{V}$. This is the "heavy lifting" done once.
    Note on Operator Assembly: Computing $\mathbf{K}_{red}$ ($\mathbf{V}^T \times \mathbf{K} \times \mathbf{V}$) yields a tiny $m \times m$ matrix that is parameter-independent, meaning we precompute it completely off-line.
  • 2. Online Phase (The Muscle):
    As you drag the load $\mathbf{F}(\xi)$ in the browser, we evaluate: $$\mathbf{F}_{red} = \mathbf{V}^T \mathbf{F}(\xi) \quad ; \quad \mathbf{K}_{red} \mathbf{q} = \mathbf{F}_{red} \quad ; \quad \mathbf{x}_{sim} = \mathbf{V} \mathbf{q}$$ The Caveat: The reduced load vector $\mathbf{F}_{red}(\mu)$ cannot always be strictly precomputed if the force depends heavily on the parameters (e.g., moving point loads). This means we still have to evaluate the full vector online just to project it, which can be slow!

Error Residual Tracking

We quantify the success of the reduction using the Relative Reconstruction Error:

$$e_{rel} = \frac{\|\mathbf{u}_{full} - \mathbf{V}\mathbf{q}\|}{\|\mathbf{u}_{full}\|} < 10^{-4}$$

This provides mathematical assurance that the browser's "fast" simulation is indistinguishable from the high-fidelity analytical solution.

Deep Learning: Autoencoders for Non-Linearity

When geometric non-linearity makes linear POD inaccurate, we replace the basis $\mathbf{V}$ with a non-linear Encoder-Decoder structure:

$\mathbf{u}(\mu) \approx \mathcal{D}(\mathbf{q}, \mu)$
$\mathbf{q}$: Latent coordinates in a manifold learned by a neural network.

Point Loads & Shape Function Locality

In standard FEM, point loads must coincide with nodes. In the Phase 1.7 IGA engine, forces can be applied at any continuous parametric coordinate $\xi \in [0, 1]$ via the Dirac Delta Distribution:

$$f_i = \int_{0}^{1} \mathbf{P} \cdot \delta(x(\xi) - \mu) R_i(\xi) J(\xi) \, d\xi = \mathbf{P} \, R_i(\mu) \, J(\mu)$$

This allows the simulated "hand" or "load" to move smoothly across the bridge. The load is distributed consistently across all active control points in the span, weighted by their influence at $\mu$. This avoids the artificial "stiffness spikes" found in nodal-constrained FEM.

Sparse Vectors via Local Support

Because Finite Element or B-spline shape functions have local support, a force applied at a specific point only activates a small subset of the degrees of freedom (DOFs).

When dealing with a moving point load $x(\mu)$, the full load vector $\mathbf{F}(\mu)$ is mostly zeros: $\mathbf{F} = [0, \dots, 0, F_i, F_{i+1}, \dots, F_{i+p}, 0, \dots, 0]^T$. Only the $p+1$ shape functions overlapping the load location become active (non-zero).

Hyper-reduction (Reduced ON)

As seen in the ROM section caveat, computing the full online vector $\mathbf{F}(\mu)$ and projecting it as $\mathbf{V}^T \mathbf{F}(\mu)$ wastes time because most of $\mathbf{F}$ is zero. Hyper-reduction solves this.

Visual: Matrix Extraction for Hyper-reduction

Selective Extraction

Instead of doing the full $m \times N$ by $N \times 1$ matrix multiplication, hyper-reduction dynamically extracts only the "concerned" columns of $\mathbf{V}^T$ and the non-zero rows of $\mathbf{F}$.

$$\mathbf{F}_{red} \approx (\mathbf{V}^T)_{extracted} \times \mathbf{F}_{non-zeros}$$

This transforms the projection complexity from $O(m \times N)$ down to $O(m \times p)$, leading to ultra-fast computation!

Neural Network / Regression Alternative

Another hyper-reduction approach bypasses the physics-based projection entirely. Once offline snapshots are collected, we can train a surrogate model (like a Neural Network) to map the parameters $\mu$ directly to the reduced coordinates $\mathbf{q}$.

$$\mu \xrightarrow{\text{ NN or Regression }} \mathbf{q} \implies \mathbf{u} \approx \mathbf{V}\mathbf{q}$$

Parameter Scaling: The Curse of Dimensionality

When generating our offline basis, we must sample the parameter space. If we use a uniform grid, the number of required calculations grows exponentially with the number of parameters $m$.

  • 1 Parameter ($\mu_1$): 1D space. Requires $p$ calculations (e.g., 10 points).
  • 2 Parameters ($\mu_1, \mu_2$): 2D space. Requires $p^2$ calculations (e.g., 100 points).
  • $m$ Parameters: Requires $p^m$ calculations. Growth is catastrophic!

Consider a simple beam with 4 parameters:
$\mu_1$ (Load Position), $\mu_2$ (Deflection Read Point), $\mu_3$ (Young's Modulus $E$), $\mu_4$ (Moment of Inertia $I$).
A standard grid of 10 points per parameter means $10^4 = 10,000$ massive FOM solves off-line!

Solution: Design of Experiments (DOE)

Instead of a full grid search, advanced methods like Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) or Sparse Grids are used to efficiently explore high-dimensional spaces with far fewer evaluations while maintaining a statistically representative basis.

Mapping Optimization: Greville Abscissae

For the Jacobian $J$ to remain stable and ideally constant ($J=1.0$) across the domain, control points must be distributed according to their Greville Abscissae (knot averages).

$$x_i^* = \frac{1}{p} \sum_{k=1}^p \xi_{i+k}$$

Distributing points at these coordinates ensures that the physical space $x$ is a linear mapping of the parametric space $\xi$. In Phase 1.7, this is critical because the flexural stiffness depends on $1/J^3$. If $J$ fluctuates wildly (non-uniform mapping), the numerical integration requires exponential sampling to converge. Greville mapping stabilizes the solve.

Strain & Stress: The B-Matrix Chain Rule

To calculate internal stresses, the engine must differentiate the displacement field $\mathbf{u}(\xi)$ with respect to physical space $x$. This requires the NURBS Basis Derivatives combined with the Jacobian inverse:

$$\varepsilon = \frac{d\mathbf{u}}{dx} = \frac{d\mathbf{u}}{d\xi} \frac{d\xi}{dx} = \frac{1}{J} \sum_{i=1}^n R'_i(\xi) u_i$$

This defines the Strain-Displacement Matrix ($\mathbf{B}$). In IGA, since the basis functions $R_i$ are $C^{p-1}$ continuous, the resulting strain and stress fields are high-order smooth, unlike the "staircase" stress jumps seen in linear FEM.

Structural Dynamics & Newmark Stability

Real-time vibration is modeled using the Generalized Equation of Motion, introducing inertial and dissipative forces:

$$\mathbf{M} \mathbf{\ddot{u}}(t) + \mathbf{C} \mathbf{\dot{u}}(t) + \mathbf{K} \mathbf{u}(t) = \mathbf{F}(t)$$

Rayleigh Proportional Damping

Energy dissipation is modeled via linear combination of the mass and stiffness matrices: $$\mathbf{C} = \alpha \mathbf{M} + \beta \mathbf{K}$$ This allows us to tune the simulation for Critical Damping at specific frequencies $\omega_i$, ensuring the bridge doesn't vibrate infinitely in the browser.

Numerical Integration: Newmark-$\beta$

We use the Average Acceleration Method ($\gamma=0.5, \beta=0.25$), which is unconditionally stable. The state at $t+\Delta t$ is solved from the previous state:

$$\mathbf{u}_{t+\Delta t} = \mathbf{u}_t + \Delta t \mathbf{\dot{u}}_t + \frac{\Delta t^2}{2} [ (1-2\beta)\mathbf{\ddot{u}}_t + 2\beta\mathbf{\ddot{u}}_{t+\Delta t} ]$$

By re-arranging this into an Effective Stiffness Matrix $\hat{\mathbf{K}}$, the computer only performs a single linear solve per timeframe.

Numerical Integration: Gauss-Legendre Quadrature

Since IGA stiffness terms involve rational basis functions $R_i(\xi)$, they cannot be integrated analytically. The Phase 1.7 engine uses **Gauss-Legendre Quadrature** to sum the contributions across each knot span:

$$\int_{\xi_k}^{\xi_{k+1}} f(\xi) \, d\xi \approx \sum_{g=1}^{n_g} w_g f(\xi_g)$$

For a degree $p$ spline, we use $n_g = p+1$ integration points to ensure exact integration of the resulting polynomial terms. This quadrature is applied element-by-element, where each non-empty knot span $[ \xi_k, \xi_{k+1} ]$ is treated as an independent IGA element.

The Mapping Problem

Gauss weights ($w_g$) and points ($\xi_g$) are tabulated and known only for a standardized parent space interval, typically $[-1, 1]$. We don't know their values for an arbitrary knot span $[\xi_k, \xi_{k+1}]$ in our actual parametric space.

Solution: A linear mapping bridges the arbitrary interval to the standardized interval. This is an affine transformation: $$\xi(\hat{\xi}) = \frac{\xi_{k+1} - \xi_k}{2} \hat{\xi} + \frac{\xi_{k+1} + \xi_k}{2}$$

We map the known coordinates from the parent space $\hat{\xi}$ to the arbitrary parametric space $\xi$ and scale the integral by the mapping derivative.

Refinement Strategies: h and p Refinement

To improve the convergence of the simulation (as seen in Phase 1.8), IGA utilizes two distinct refinement operators that preserve the exact geometry while increasing the solution space.

h-Refinement (Knot Insertion)

New knots $\hat{\xi}$ are inserted into the knot vector using Boehm’s Algorithm. This adds "resolution" locally to the bridge without changing its overall shape: $$\hat{P}_i = \alpha_i P_i + (1 - \alpha_i) P_{i-1} \quad ; \quad \alpha_i = \frac{\hat{\xi} - \xi_i}{\xi_{i+p} - \xi_i}$$

p-Refinement (Degree Elevation)

Elevation increases the polynomial degree from $p$ to $p+1$. This enhances the smooth continuity between elements. For rational curves (like our circle), this requires Homogeneous Least-Squares mapping to ensure the curve radius remains constant at $R=1$ while using higher-order basis functions.

Phase 3.5b | Advanced Hyper-Reduction

U-DEIM
Unassembled DEIM

Breaking the assembly bottleneck through element-level sparse interpolation.

1. The Assembly Problem

In standard DEIM (Phase 3.4a), we approximate the assembled internal force vector $\mathbf{F}_{int} \in \mathbb{R}^N$. However, evaluating even a single entry of $\mathbf{F}_{int}$ often requires visiting multiple elements that share that DOF. For complex nonlinearities, the cost of "assembling just what we need" can still be significant.

U-DEIM (Unassembled DEIM) shifts the focus from the assembled global vector to the unassembled force vector $\mathcal{F} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_e \times n_{dof}^e}$:

$$\mathcal{F}(\mathbf{u}) = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{f}_1(\mathbf{u}_1) \\ \mathbf{f}_2(\mathbf{u}_2) \\ \vdots \\ \mathbf{f}_{n_e}(\mathbf{u}_{n_e}) \end{bmatrix}$$

where $\mathbf{f}_e$ is the local force contribution of element $e$. By approximating $\mathcal{F}$ directly, we can skip the assembly process entirely for most elements.

2. The U-DEIM Formulation

We approximate the unassembled force field using a POD basis $\mathbf{U}_{\mathcal{F}}$ built from unassembled force snapshots:

$$\mathcal{F} \approx \mathbf{U}_{\mathcal{F}} \left( \mathbf{P}^T \mathbf{U}_{\mathcal{F}} \right)^{-1} \mathbf{P}^T \mathcal{F}$$

The key difference is that $\mathbf{P}$ now selects specific integration points or local DOFs within elements. This allows the solver to jump directly to the $m$ most important integration points across the mesh.

3. Role of Polynomial Degree ($p$)

In Isogeometric Analysis (IGA), the polynomial degree $p$ has a profound impact on hyper-reduction:

  • Support Span: Higher $p$ increases the overlap (support) of basis functions. This makes the force field smoother but also increases the number of DOFs affected by a single U-DEIM point.
  • Integration Points: For a degree $p$, we typically use $(p+1)^2$ Gauss points per element. U-DEIM must select points within these local grids.
  • Accuracy: Higher $p$ generally leads to better approximation of the nonlinear manifold, allowing U-DEIM to converge with fewer points $m$.

The Limitation of Linear Statics

In the standard linear IGA solver from Phase 1.7, the governing equation is:

$$\mathbf{K}_{lin} \mathbf{u} = \mathbf{F}$$

The stiffness matrix $\mathbf{K}_{lin}$ is assembled once and never changes. This is valid for small deflections. However, when the applied load $\mathbf{F}$ is large enough that the beam deflects significantly, the geometry itself changes — the beam stretches, its arc length increases, and internal axial tension develops.

The linear solver is completely blind to these effects. If you push an immense force onto a cantilevered beam, the linear math will happily stretch the tip infinitely downward in a straight line. It will never curl, stiffen, or behave like a real physical structure because $\mathbf{K}_{lin}$ remains frozen at the undeformed configuration.

The Geometric Stiffness Matrix ($\mathbf{K}_{geom}$)

When a beam bends significantly, its centerline stretches. This stretching generates internal axial tension $N_{ax}$ that resists further bending — a phenomenon called stress stiffening. This is the von Kármán approximation for geometric non-linearity.

1 Compute the Induced Axial Force

The slope $v'(\xi)$ of the current deformed shape is evaluated using the IGA basis function derivatives. The induced axial tension is computed from the stretching energy:

$$N_{ax} = \frac{EA}{2L} \int_0^1 \left( v'(\xi) \right)^2 d\xi$$

2 Assemble $\mathbf{K}_{geom}$

The geometric stiffness matrix is assembled using the first derivatives of the IGA basis functions, scaled by the induced axial force $N_{ax}$:

$$(K_{geom})_{ij} = \int_0^1 N_{ax} \frac{dR_i}{d\xi} \frac{dR_j}{d\xi} d\xi$$

This matrix grows larger as the beam bends more — it's the mathematical representation of "the more you bend, the stiffer you get."

Membrane Action (Stretching)

Because the beam is physically constrained at its ends (Clamped or Pinned), it cannot simply slide inward as it deflects. To form a curve, it must stretch. This stretching induces a uniform axial tension throughout the beam cross-section. This is why for very large deflections, the beam begins to behave more like a tensioned cable than a traditional bending beam.

Newton-Raphson Iteration

The non-linear equilibrium equation becomes:

$$\mathbf{K}_T(\mathbf{u}) \, \Delta\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{R}(\mathbf{u})$$

where $\mathbf{K}_T = \mathbf{K}_{lin} + \mathbf{K}_{geom}(\mathbf{u})$ is the Tangent Stiffness and $\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{F}_{ext} - \mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{u})$ is the Residual Force (the out-of-balance force). This is solved iteratively:

1 Assemble Tangent Stiffness

Build $\mathbf{K}_T(\mathbf{u})$ from the current deformed shape.

2 Compute Internal Force

$\mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{u}) = \mathbf{K}_{lin} \mathbf{u} + \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{K}_{geom}(\mathbf{u}) \mathbf{u}$

3 Compute Residual

$\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{F}_{ext} - \mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{u})$

4 Solve for Increment

$\Delta\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{K}_T^{-1} \mathbf{R}$

5 Update & Check Convergence

$\mathbf{u} \leftarrow \mathbf{u} + \Delta\mathbf{u}$. Repeat until $\|\Delta\mathbf{u}\| < \epsilon$.

Numerical Strategies Compared

The NL App allows you to compare different numerical strategies to solve the non-linear equation. Each has trade-offs between speed, stability, and accuracy per iteration:

Strategy Stiffness Update ($\mathbf{K}_{curr}$) Convergence Speed
Full Newton Updated every iteration Quadratic. Finds the exact Jacobian $\mathbf{K}_L + 3\mathbf{K}_g$.
Modified Newton Updated once per load step Linear. Reuses the same tangent for all iterations in a step.
Picard (Secant) Secant / Lagged Stiffness Linear. Uses the secant matrix $\mathbf{K}_L + \mathbf{K}_g$ (half-stiffness). Very stable.
Incremental No iterations (Euler) Approximation. Purely tangential, prone to drift.

The Newton-Raphson Jacobian

Because the internal force $\mathbf{F}_{int} = (\mathbf{K}_L + \mathbf{K}_g)\mathbf{u}$ is cubic, its derivative (the Tangent) results in a factor of 3 for the geometric term: $\mathbf{K}_T = \mathbf{K}_L + 3\mathbf{K}_g$. Our solver implements this exact Jacobian for "Full Newton" to achieve machine-precision convergence in just 3-4 steps.

Observation Goal

Notice that while Newton-Raphson converges in 3-5 iterations for most loads, the Incremental method results in a curve that "drifts" away from the true equilibrium because it never corrects the tangent error.

Fiber Stress & Curvature

Once the displacement field $\mathbf{u}$ is found, we evaluate the internal state of the material. In IGA bending, we focus on the longitudinal fiber stress:

$$\sigma_{total}(\xi, y) = \underbrace{E \cdot y \cdot \kappa(\xi)}_{\text{Bending}} + \underbrace{E \cdot \epsilon_m}_{\text{Membrane}}$$

where $\kappa(\xi)$ is the curvature ($v''$) and $\epsilon_m$ is the induced membrane strain.

Symmetry Breaking

While bending stress is symmetric (Tension on one side, Compression on the other), Membrane Stress is always Tensile. This means that for a downward load, the total tension at the bottom fiber is amplified, while the compression at the top fiber is partially cancelled out by the stretching effect.

Stress Visualization

In the Analysis Tab, the beam is color-coded using a diverging scale: Red indicates high tensile stress, Blue indicates high compressive stress, and White represents the neutral axis (zero stress). You can toggle between the Top and Bottom fibers to see the coexisting states.

Convergence & Residuals

The quality of a non-linear solution is measured by the Residual Norm $\|\mathbf{R}\|$. This represents the "out-of-balance" force that the solver must eliminate to reach equilibrium.

$$\|\mathbf{R}\| = \sqrt{\sum (F_{ext} - F_{int})^2}$$

In the Metrics Tab, we plot the logarithm of this norm over iterations. A truly quadratic solver (Newton) will show a steep downward "cliff," whereas linear solvers (Picard) show a constant slope.

Path-Following & P-Delta

Complex non-linearities often require Incremental Loading. Instead of applying 100% of the force at once, we apply it in small steps $\Delta F$. This ensures the solver stays close to the equilibrium path.

$$\mathbf{F}_{step} = \lambda \cdot \mathbf{F}_{final} \quad \text{where } \lambda \in [0, 1]$$

Plotting the maximum displacement against the load factor $\lambda$ produces the P-Delta Curve. In our app, you will see a "stiffening" curve where the slope increases as axial tension pulls the beam back, resisting further bending.

Important: Boundary Dependence

Non-linear stiffening (the "curve") only occurs if the beam's ends are **axially constrained** (e.g. Clamped-Clamped). In a simple Cantilever (Free end), the beam can bend without stretching, resulting in a nearly linear P-Delta response. Switch to "Fixed-Fixed" BCs to see the logarithmic stiffening curve!

Non-Linear Reduced Order Model (NL-ROM)

In the linear ROM (Phase 1.7), Galerkin Projection maps the system into a modal subspace once. For non-linear problems, the Tangent Stiffness $\mathbf{K}_T$ changes every iteration — so the projection must be repeated at every Newton-Raphson step.

Step Full NL-IGA ($N \times N$) NL-ROM ($m \times m$)
Build Tangent $\mathbf{K}_T(\mathbf{u})$ at full DOF $\mathbf{K}_T(\mathbf{\Phi q}_r)$ then project
Project Not needed — already full $\mathbf{K}_{red-T} = \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{K}_T \mathbf{\Phi}$
Solve Gaussian Elimination ($O(N^3)$) $\mathbf{K}_{red-T} \Delta \mathbf{q}_r = \mathbf{R}_{red}$
Reconstruct Direct physical DOFs $\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{q}_r$

The Key Insight

Even though we must rebuild $\mathbf{K}_T$ in full space each iteration (because the geometry-dependent $\mathbf{K}_{geom}$ requires the full displacement field), the actual solve operates on the tiny $[3 \times 3]$ reduced system. This means each iteration costs roughly $O(N^2)$ for projection but only $O(m^3)$ for the solve — a dramatic speedup when $m \ll N$.

NL-ROM Iteration Loop:

$$\mathbf{u}^{(k)} = \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{q}_r^{(k)}$$ $$\mathbf{K}_{red-T}^{(k)} = \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{K}_T(\mathbf{u}^{(k)}) \mathbf{\Phi}$$ $$\mathbf{R}_{red}^{(k)} = \mathbf{\Phi}^T \left( \mathbf{F}_{ext} - \mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{u}^{(k)}) \right)$$ $$\mathbf{K}_{red-T}^{(k)} \Delta \mathbf{q}_r = \mathbf{R}_{red}^{(k)}$$ $$\mathbf{q}_r^{(k+1)} = \mathbf{q}_r^{(k)} + \Delta \mathbf{q}_r$$

NL-ROM Iteration Loop:

$$\mathbf{u}^{(k)} = \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{q}_r^{(k)}$$ $$\mathbf{K}_{red-T}^{(k)} = \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{K}_T(\mathbf{u}^{(k)}) \mathbf{\Phi}$$ $$\mathbf{R}_{red}^{(k)} = \mathbf{\Phi}^T \left( \mathbf{F}_{ext} - \mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{u}^{(k)}) \right)$$ $$\mathbf{K}_{red-T}^{(k)} \Delta \mathbf{q}_r = \mathbf{R}_{red}^{(k)}$$ $$\mathbf{q}_r^{(k+1)} = \mathbf{q}_r^{(k)} + \Delta \mathbf{q}_r$$

ROM Mode Basis Selection

The Non-Linear ROM requires a set of modes $\Phi$ that capture both the linear behavior and the non-linear curvature. The app allows you to choose between two extraction methods:

Physical IGA Modes (Numerical EVD)

The app solves the discrete eigenvalue problem $\mathbf{K}_{lin} \mathbf{\phi} = \lambda \mathbf{M} \mathbf{\phi}$ (using Power Iteration + Deflation) to find the exact free-vibration mode shapes of the current basis. Benefit: Perfectly matches the IGA reference model.

Approximate Analytic (Trigonometric)

Uses analytical sine or cosine-based functions that are manually adjusted to satisfy boundary conditions (e.g. zero-slope at clamped supports). Benefit: Extremely fast, no numerical solve required.

Torque-to-Circle (Closed-Form Bypass)

Phase 1.7 also includes a closed-form analytical solution for the special case of a pure uniform bending moment $M$. When the moment is constant along the beam, the curvature $\kappa$ is also constant, producing a perfect circular arc:

$$\kappa = \frac{M}{EI} \implies r = \frac{1}{\kappa}$$

This bypasses the Newton-Raphson loop entirely and maps control points directly onto a circle. It serves as a visual validation tool — if the NL solver is correct, applying a uniform moment should produce a shape that closely matches this exact circular geometry.

Parametrized Domains & Master Reference

When extending Non-Linear IGA beyond 1D beams to 2D continuous structures (like a parametrized "dogbone" tensile specimen), we face a new problem: the geometry boundaries themselves move as parameters change.

The Moving Boundary Challenge

If the parameter $\mu_1$ controls the "neck radius" of the dogbone, then changing $\mu_1$ physically alters the domain $\Omega(\mu)$. This means the mesh nodes change position for every new parameter value, making standard ROM techniques (which require a fixed nodal frame of reference) fail completely.

The IGA Master Domain Solution

IGA solves this elegantly. Instead of formulating the problem on the moving physical domain $\Omega(\mu)$, we pull everything back to a fixed Master Reference Domain $\hat{\Omega}$ (the parametric space $\xi_1, \xi_2$).

$$\mathbf{x}(\xi, \mu) = \sum_{i=1}^n R_i(\xi) \mathbf{P}_i(\mu)$$

The control points $\mathbf{P}_i$ become functions of the parameters $\mu$, but the integration domain $\hat{\Omega}$ remains perfectly static!

This requires us to carefully track the Deformation Gradient/Jacobian ($\mathbf{J}$). Because the mapping from $\hat{\Omega}$ to physical space now depends on $\mu$, the stiffness matrix generation must incorporate the parameter-dependent Jacobian at every integration point. While this makes offline assembly more complex, it guarantees an exact geometric representation of parameterized shapes without ever needing to remesh.

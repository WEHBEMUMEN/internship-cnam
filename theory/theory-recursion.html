METHODOLOGY

Real-Time Recursion

How does the browser calculate a complex physical shape 60 times a second without any lag? The secret lies in a mathematical technique called Recursion, specifically the Cox-de Boor algorithm.

The Waterfall Analogy

Imagine a giant waterfall with many different pools at different levels. Water from the top pool flows down into several smaller pools below it. To calculate the final shape of the curve, our engine "follows the water" from the simple building blocks at the top down to the final smooth curve at the bottom. Our engine is so fast that it can track millions of "droplets" (calculations) per second.

1. The Cox-de Boor Algorithm

The Cox-de Boor formula is used to calculate the basis functions ($N_{i,p}$) of any B-Spline. It is recursive because to find the value at degree $p$, it must first solve the values for $p-1$.

N[i, 2] --↘
          N[i, 3] (Smooth Cube)
N[i+1, 2] --↗

(Higher levels depend on the levels below them)

By using a specific computational structure, we convert what looks like a mountain of math into a simple triangular scheme. This reduces the work from an "exponential" nightmare to a fast "polynomial" update.

O(p²) Complexity

Our implementation is mathematically lean. For a cubic spline, it only takes roughly 9 operations per point to evaluate the whole shape.

Zero Allocation

During simulation, we avoid creating new "objects" in memory. By reusing the same numbers, we prevent the browser's "Garbage Collector" from causing stuttering or lag.

2. Interaction Latency (The 16ms Budget)

For a Digital Twin to feel real, it must update in under 16 milliseconds. This is the window of time our eyes perceive as "instant."

Even with 500 sampling points and complex physical stiffness matrices, our Cox-de Boor implementation completes in less than **2 milliseconds**. This leaves plenty of room for heavy 2D/3D physics in future phases!

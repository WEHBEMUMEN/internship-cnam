1. Evolution of the Solver Architecture

Our computational core has undergone a major shift from a server-side dependency to a Native Client-Side Engine. This evolution was necessary to achieve the real-time performance goals of a true Digital Twin.

The Previous Method

Originally, we used a FastAPI + Nutils backend. The browser sent the NURBS geometry to a Python server, which solved the system and returned the results.

  • Network Latency: Round-trip delay made real-time interaction impossible.
  • Infrastructure Load: High setup complexity requiring a persistent Python environment.
  • Sync Issues: Keeping the JS UI state and Python solver topology in sync was error-prone.

The New Implementation

We have transitioned to a Pure JavaScript High-Fidelity Solver using custom-developed IGA classes that run directly in the user's browser.

  • Instant Feedback: 0ms network latency; computations happen locally.
  • Zero Setup: Runs on any modern browser without needing a backend server.
  • Total Control: The logic is unified in a single language (JS), allowing deeper integration.

2. Native IGA Assembly Logic

The new JavaScript solver implements the full **Galerkin Method** pipeline for 2D NURBS patches. It handles the mapping from parametric space to physical space analytically:

$$\mathbf{K} = \sum_{e} \int_{\Omega_e} \mathbf{B}^T \mathbf{D} \mathbf{B} \det(\mathbf{J}) d\Omega$$

By leveraging Gaussian Quadrature (typically $p+1$ points), we integrate the material stiffness across every knot span. The B-matrix is constructed using analytical NURBS derivatives, ensuring that the "Isogeometric" principle—using the same basis for geometry and physics—is strictly maintained.

3. Scaling for Large Clusters

To solve the resulting linear system $\mathbf{K}\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{f}$, we use efficient matrix reduction techniques that mask Dirichlet boundary conditions and solve for the unknown displacements using stabilized Gaussian elimination.

$$\begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{K}_{ff} & \mathbf{K}_{fc} \\ \mathbf{K}_{cf} & \mathbf{K}_{cc} \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} \mathbf{u}_f \\ \mathbf{u}_c \end{Bmatrix} = \begin{Bmatrix} \mathbf{f}_f \\ \mathbf{f}_c \end{Bmatrix}$$

This allows us to maintain high precision even as the control point lattice grows, providing the numerical stability required for benchmarking against analytical solutions.

4. Numerical Stability & The "Overlapping Points" Issue

One of the most significant challenges encountered during the implementation of the Kirsch benchmark was the Issue of Overlapping Control Points. In NURBS-based IGA, degenerate or "collapsed" mappings are often used to create sharp corners, but they introduce severe numerical artifacts.

The Problem: Coincident Points

Placing two control points at the exact same spatial coordinate (e.g., to force a $C^0$ sharp corner) causes the local area element to collapse to zero.

$$\det(\mathbf{J}) = 0 \implies \text{Stiffness } K = \infty$$

Impact: This caused Numerical Locking. Even under massive loads, the nodes at these locations appeared "frozen" or "blue" (zero displacement) because the computer treated their stiffness as infinite.

The Solution: Penalty Constraints

While a "Micro-Nudge" was initially considered, the most robust solution is the Penalty Method. We identify points that occupy the same spatial coordinate and mathematically "tie" their Degrees of Freedom together:

$$K_{penalty} = 10^{12} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Result: By adding a massive stiffness between the coincident nodes, the solver is forced to give them nearly identical displacements. This resolves the singularity while maintaining the perfect geometry of the sharp corner.

5. Boundary Traction Integration

In classical FEM, nodal loads are often applied using simple "tributary areas." In Isogeometric Analysis (IGA), where meshes are frequently non-uniform or higher-order, we must use Boundary Integration to ensure a perfectly uniform traction distribution.

$$\mathbf{f}_i^{\text{ext}} = \int_{\Gamma_N} R_i(\xi) \mathbf{T}(\xi) \det(\mathbf{J}_{1D}) d\xi$$

By integrating the traction $\mathbf{T}$ over the basis functions $R_i$, the solver automatically "weights" the nodal forces based on the physical size of the elements. This prevents "force clustering" in refined areas and ensures the total applied load matches the theoretical requirement exactly.

6. Evolution of Force Assembly

Our journey to a physically rigorous traction load involved three distinct stages of refinement:

Stage 1: Simple Nodal Loads

Applying equal forces to each node. Inaccurate for non-uniform meshes; causes "force clustering" in dense regions.

Stage 2: Geometric Manual Weighting

Manually halving the force at the corners. Better, but failed to account for NURBS parameter non-uniformity and degenerate corner locking.

Stage 3: Integrated Boundary Assembly (Current)

Using Gaussian quadrature to integrate traction over shape functions. Mathematically exact for any mesh density.

7. Numerical Algorithm (Appendix 4.C)

To implement the integrated traction load, we follow standard element-assembly logic:

FOR each boundary element e DO
   FOR each quadrature point i DO
      Evaluate Basis Functions (Na)
      Calculate Jacobian Modifier (Jmod = J * gw[i])
      
      FOR each local basis function aa DO
         f_local[aa] += load * Na * Jmod
      END FOR
   END FOR
   Assemble f_local into Global Force Vector f_ext
END FOR

Note: The penalty constraints applied to the coincident corner points ensure the matrix remains non-singular while keeping the assembly topologically exact.

8. Analytical Solution (Stress Benchmarking)

For the benchmark problem of an infinite plate with a circular hole of radius $R$, the exact analytical stress field under uniaxial tension $T_x$ at infinity is defined in polar coordinates $(r, \theta)$:

Radial Stress: $$\sigma_{rr}(r,\theta) = \frac{T_x}{2} \left[ \left(1 - \frac{R^2}{r^2}\right) + \left(1 - 4\frac{R^2}{r^2} + 3\frac{R^4}{r^4}\right) \cos 2\theta \right]$$
Hoop Stress: $$\sigma_{\theta\theta}(r,\theta) = \frac{T_x}{2} \left[ \left(1 + \frac{R^2}{r^2}\right) - \left(1 + 3\frac{R^4}{r^4}\right) \cos 2\theta \right]$$
Shear Stress: $$\sigma_{r\theta}(r,\theta) = -\frac{T_x}{2} \left(1 + 2\frac{R^2}{r^2} - 3\frac{R^4}{r^4}\right) \sin 2\theta$$

These equations serve as the "ground truth" to which our NURBS-based numerical solution is compared during the benchmarking process.

9. $L_2$-Norm Error & Convergence

To rigorously evaluate accuracy, we compute the Relative $L_2$-Norm of Stress Error. This measures the total energy difference between the numerical ($\sigma_{num}$) and exact ($\sigma_{exact}$) fields over the entire domain $\Omega$:

$$\|e\|_{L_2} = \frac{\sqrt{\int_{\Omega} (\sigma_{num} - \sigma_{exact})^2 d\Omega}}{\sqrt{\int_{\Omega} \sigma_{exact}^2 d\Omega}}$$

Convergence Rates

As the mesh size $h_{max}$ decreases, the error should drop at an optimal rate related to the polynomial degree $p$ of the NURBS basis functions:

  • Quadratic ($p=2$): Error $\approx \mathcal{O}(h^2)$
  • Cubic ($p=3$): Error $\approx \mathcal{O}(h^3)$
  • Quartic ($p=4$): Error $\approx \mathcal{O}(h^4)$

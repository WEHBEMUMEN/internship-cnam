Newton-Raphson

Large deformations and the iterative path to equilibrium.

1. Geometric Nonlinearity

When a structure undergoes large rotations or displacements, the linear strain assumption ($\epsilon = \frac{1}{2}(\nabla u + \nabla u^T)$) is no longer valid. We must use the **Green-Lagrange Strain Tensor** $E$, which accounts for the quadratic terms of deformation:

$$E = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{F}^T \mathbf{F} - \mathbf{I})$$ $$E_{ij} = \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial X_j} + \frac{\partial u_j}{\partial X_i} + \frac{\partial u_k}{\partial X_i} \frac{\partial u_k}{\partial X_j} \right)$$

where $\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{I} + \nabla \mathbf{u}$ is the deformation gradient. This allows us to track the stiffness change caused by the current shape of the structure.

2. The Newton-Raphson Loop

Since the equilibrium equation $F_{ext} = F_{int}(u)$ is now nonlinear, we cannot solve for $u$ in a single step. We use the **Newton-Raphson method**, which linearizes the problem at each iteration $k$:

$$\mathbf{K}_t(\mathbf{u}_k) \Delta \mathbf{u} = \mathbf{F}_{ext} - \mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{u}_k)$$ $$\mathbf{u}_{k+1} = \mathbf{u}_k + \Delta \mathbf{u}$$
  • $\mathbf{K}_t$ is the **Tangent Stiffness Matrix**, composed of material and geometric parts.
  • $\mathbf{R} = \mathbf{F}_{ext} - \mathbf{F}_{int}$ is the **Residual Force**, representing the out-of-equilibrium error.

3. Tangent Stiffness Components

The tangent stiffness $\mathbf{K}_t$ is split into two physical effects:

$$\mathbf{K}_t = \mathbf{K}_{mat} + \mathbf{K}_{geo}$$
  • $\mathbf{K}_{mat}$: The standard stiffness, but evaluated at the current deformed state.
  • $\mathbf{K}_{geo}$: The **Geometric Stiffness** (or stress-stiffening), which accounts for how internal stresses resist or assist further deformation (like a guitar string becoming stiffer when pulled).

4. Numerical Stability & Jacobian Locking

A common issue in Isogeometric Analysis (IGA) occurs at corners where multiple control points are coincident (degenerate mapping). While this ensures a sharp geometric corner, it creates a Singular Jacobian.

The Old Method (Singular)

Placing multiple control points at the exact same coordinate $(L, L)$ causes the area element to vanish:

$$\det(\mathbf{J}) \to 0 \implies \mathbf{K} \propto \frac{1}{\det(\mathbf{J})} \to \infty$$

Result: Stiffness Locking. The boundary nodes appear "blue" (fixed) because their stiffness is numerically infinite.

The New Solution (Nudged)

We apply a tiny Micro-Nudge ($0.01\,\text{mm}$) to the redundant points. This restores a valid mapping while maintaining visual accuracy:

$$\det(\mathbf{J}) > \epsilon \implies \mathbf{K} < \text{Tol}$$

Result: Physical Movement. The boundary is now flexible and correctly responds to traction loads.

5. Boundary Traction Integration

In classical FEM, nodal loads are often applied using simple "tributary areas." In Isogeometric Analysis (IGA), where meshes are frequently non-uniform or higher-order, we must use Boundary Integration to ensure a perfectly uniform traction distribution.

$$\mathbf{f}_i^{\text{ext}} = \int_{\Gamma_N} R_i(\xi) \mathbf{T}(\xi) \det(\mathbf{J}_{1D}) d\xi$$

By integrating the traction $\mathbf{T}$ over the basis functions $R_i$, the solver automatically "weights" the nodal forces based on the physical size of the elements. This prevents "force clustering" in refined areas and ensures the total applied load matches the theoretical requirement exactly.

6. Evolution of Force Assembly

Our journey to a physically rigorous traction load involved three distinct stages of refinement:

Stage 1: Simple Nodal Loads

Applying equal forces to each node. Inaccurate for non-uniform meshes; causes "force clustering" in dense regions.

Stage 2: Geometric Manual Weighting

Manually halving the force at the corners. Better, but failed to account for NURBS parameter non-uniformity and degenerate corner locking.

Stage 3: Integrated Boundary Assembly (Current)

Using Gaussian quadrature to integrate traction over shape functions. Mathematically exact for any mesh density.

7. Numerical Algorithm (Appendix 4.C)

To implement the integrated traction load, we follow standard element-assembly logic:

FOR each boundary element e DO
   FOR each quadrature point i DO
      Evaluate Basis Functions (Na)
      Calculate Jacobian Modifier (Jmod = J * gw[i])
      
      FOR each local basis function aa DO
         f_local[aa] += load * Na * Jmod
      END FOR
   END FOR
   Assemble f_local into Global Force Vector f_ext
END FOR

Note: The top-right corner nudge ensures $\det(\mathbf{J}) > 0$, preventing matrix singularity while keeping the assembly topologically exact.

8. Analytical Solution (Stress Benchmarking)

For the benchmark problem of an infinite plate with a circular hole of radius $R$, the exact analytical stress field under uniaxial tension $T_x$ at infinity is defined in polar coordinates $(r, \theta)$:

Radial Stress: $$\sigma_{rr}(r,\theta) = \frac{T_x}{2} \left[ \left(1 - \frac{R^2}{r^2}\right) + \left(1 - 4\frac{R^2}{r^2} + 3\frac{R^4}{r^4}\right) \cos 2\theta \right]$$
Hoop Stress: $$\sigma_{\theta\theta}(r,\theta) = \frac{T_x}{2} \left[ \left(1 + \frac{R^2}{r^2}\right) - \left(1 + 3\frac{R^4}{r^4}\right) \cos 2\theta \right]$$
Shear Stress: $$\sigma_{r\theta}(r,\theta) = -\frac{T_x}{2} \left(1 + 2\frac{R^2}{r^2} - 3\frac{R^4}{r^4}\right) \sin 2\theta$$

These equations serve as the "ground truth" to which our NURBS-based numerical solution is compared during the benchmarking process.

9. $L_2$-Norm Error & Convergence

To rigorously evaluate accuracy, we compute the Relative $L_2$-Norm of Stress Error. This measures the total energy difference between the numerical ($\sigma_{num}$) and exact ($\sigma_{exact}$) fields over the entire domain $\Omega$:

$$\|e\|_{L_2} = \frac{\sqrt{\int_{\Omega} (\sigma_{num} - \sigma_{exact})^2 d\Omega}}{\sqrt{\int_{\Omega} \sigma_{exact}^2 d\Omega}}$$

Convergence Rates

As the mesh size $h_{max}$ decreases, the error should drop at an optimal rate related to the polynomial degree $p$ of the NURBS basis functions:

  • Quadratic ($p=2$): Error $\approx \mathcal{O}(h^2)$
  • Cubic ($p=3$): Error $\approx \mathcal{O}(h^3)$
  • Quartic ($p=4$): Error $\approx \mathcal{O}(h^4)$

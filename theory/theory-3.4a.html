Phase 3.4a | Hyper-Reduction

DEIM
Hyper-Reduction

Discrete Empirical Interpolation Method for efficient nonlinear force approximation.

1. The Nonlinear Bottleneck

In Galerkin ROM, we project the full-order system onto a small POD basis $\Phi \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times k}$ where $k \ll N$. The reduced system becomes:

Galerkin Projection

$$\Phi^T K_T(\mathbf{u}) \Phi \, \Delta \mathbf{u}_r = \Phi^T \left( \mathbf{F}_{ext} - \mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{u}) \right)$$

The solve is now $k \times k$ — fast! But the assembly of $\mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{u})$ still requires looping over all $N$ DOFs. This is the nonlinear bottleneck: even though we solve a tiny system, we still pay $\mathcal{O}(N)$ to evaluate the internal forces at every Newton iteration.

2. The DEIM Idea

DEIM (Chaturantabut & Sorensen, 2010) approximates the nonlinear function $\mathbf{F}_{int}$ by sampling it at only $m$ carefully chosen DOF indices:

DEIM Approximation

$$\mathbf{F}_{int} \approx \tilde{\mathbf{F}}_{int} = U_f \, \mathbf{c}$$

where $U_f \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times m}$ is a POD basis of the force snapshots, and the coefficient vector $\mathbf{c} \in \mathbb{R}^m$ is determined by interpolation at the selected indices:

Interpolation Condition

$$P^T U_f \, \mathbf{c} = P^T \mathbf{F}_{int}$$

Here $P \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times m}$ is a selector matrix — column $i$ of $P$ is $\mathbf{e}_{p_i}$, the unit vector corresponding to the $i$-th DEIM interpolation index.

The interpolation indices $\{p_1, \ldots, p_m\}$ are selected greedily to maximize the captured information:

4. Online Phase

During each Newton-Raphson iteration of the online solve:

  1. Expand reduced coordinates: $\mathbf{u} = \Phi \, \mathbf{u}_r$
  2. Evaluate $\mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{u})$ — full assembly (still $\mathcal{O}(N)$ in this basic formulation)
  3. Extract sampled entries: $\mathbf{f}_p = P^T \mathbf{F}_{int}$ — only $m$ values
  4. Reconstruct: $\tilde{\mathbf{F}}_{int} = U_f (P^T U_f)^{-1} \mathbf{f}_p$
  5. Reduced residual: $\mathbf{R}_r = \Phi^T(\mathbf{F}_{ext} - \tilde{\mathbf{F}}_{int})$
  6. Reduced tangent: $K_r = \Phi^T K_T \Phi$ (still Galerkin for tangent)
  7. Solve $K_r \, \Delta\mathbf{u}_r = \mathbf{R}_r$

Key Insight

In this first implementation, DEIM approximates $\mathbf{F}_{int}$ but the tangent $K_T$ is still fully assembled. True computational savings require element-level sampling (ECSW, ECM) — covered in Phases 3.4b–3.4c.

5. Error Bound

The DEIM approximation error satisfies:

DEIM Error Bound

$$\|\mathbf{F}_{int} - \tilde{\mathbf{F}}_{int}\| \leq \|(P^T U_f)^{-1}\| \cdot \|\mathbf{F}_{int} - U_f U_f^T \mathbf{F}_{int}\|$$

The first factor depends on the conditioning of the interpolation matrix (controlled by the greedy selection). The second factor is the POD truncation error — it decays with the singular values of the force snapshot matrix.

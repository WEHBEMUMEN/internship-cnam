Phase 3.4c · Theory

Energy-Conserving Sampling
& Weighting (ECSW)

A hyper-reduction method that guarantees energy conservation through non-negative element weighting — replacing node-based DEIM interpolation.

§1 · Motivation

Why ECSW Instead of DEIM?

In DEIM (Phase 3.4a), the nonlinear internal force is reconstructed via node-based interpolation:

$$F_{int} \approx \Phi_F \left(P^T \Phi_F\right)^{-1} F_{sampled}$$
DEIM's Two Weaknesses

1. The interpolation matrix $(P^T \Phi_F)$ can become ill-conditioned, causing divergence and 15%+ errors.

2. DEIM does not conserve energy — the approximated force field may violate the work-energy theorem.

ECSW takes a fundamentally different approach. Instead of interpolating forces at selected nodes, it approximates the element integral using a weighted sum over selected elements:

$$\int_\Omega f(\mathbf{u}) \, d\Omega \;\approx\; \sum_{e \in \mathcal{Z}} w_e \int_{\Omega_e} f(\mathbf{u}) \, d\Omega_e$$

where $\mathcal{Z}$ is a sparse subset of elements and $w_e \geq 0$ are non-negative weights. The non-negativity constraint is what guarantees energy conservation.

§2 · Offline Pipeline

Stage 1: Snapshot Collection & POD Basis

Identical to DEIM — compute the SVD of displacement snapshots to get the reduced basis $\Phi \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times k}$.

// romEngine.computePOD(k) extracts Φ [nDofs × k]
const podInfo = this.romEngine.computePOD(this.k);

Stage 2: Element-wise Projected Force Matrix $G$

For each element $e$ and snapshot $s$, compute the projected element internal force:

$$G_e^{(s)} = \Phi^T \cdot f_e^{int}(\mathbf{u}_s)$$

This produces a matrix $G \in \mathbb{R}^{(k \cdot n_{snap}) \times n_{elem}}$ — each column represents one element's contribution across all snapshots.

// Each column of G is built by _computeElementProjectedForce()
const Ge = this._computeElementProjectedForce(fomSolver, patch, el, snapDisp);

Stage 3: Non-Negative Least Squares (NNLS)

The key innovation: find weights $\mathbf{w} \geq 0$ such that:

$$G \mathbf{w} \approx \mathbf{b}, \quad w_e \geq 0 \;\;\forall\, e$$

where $\mathbf{b} = \sum_e G_e$ is the total projected force. Elements with $w_e = 0$ are discarded, creating the sparse set $\mathcal{Z}$.

// NNLS solver finds optimal non-negative weights
const result = this._solveNNLS(G, b, tolerance);

Key Insight

ECSW avoids the $(P^T \Phi_F)^{-1}$ inversion entirely. There is no interpolation matrix to become ill-conditioned. The NNLS problem is always well-posed because the non-negativity constraint acts as an automatic regularizer.

§4 · Online Phase

Reduced Newton-Raphson

During the online solve, the reduced internal force and tangent stiffness are assembled only over sampled elements:

$$F_{int}^{red}(\mathbf{u}_r) = \sum_{e \in \mathcal{Z}} w_e \cdot \Phi_e^T \, f_e^{int}(\Phi \, \mathbf{u}_r)$$
$$K_t^{red}(\mathbf{u}_r) = \sum_{e \in \mathcal{Z}} w_e \cdot \Phi_e^T \, K_e^t(\Phi \, \mathbf{u}_r) \, \Phi_e$$

where $\Phi_e$ denotes the rows of $\Phi$ corresponding to the DOFs of element $e$. This is the hyper-reduction: instead of looping over all $n_{elem}$ elements, we loop over $|\mathcal{Z}| \ll n_{elem}$ elements.

The Newton-Raphson update in reduced coordinates becomes:

$$K_t^{red} \,\Delta \mathbf{u}_r = F_{ext}^{red} - F_{int}^{red}$$

This is a $k \times k$ linear system (typically $k = 5\text{–}20$), trivially cheap to solve.

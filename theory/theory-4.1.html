PHASE 4.1 THEORY

From Static to Dynamic ROM

Lessons from static hyper-reduction applied to transient structural problems.

Retrospective: What we learned from Phase 3

Our journey through static nonlinear phases (3.1–3.5) established the critical pillars for dynamic simulation:

  • The Spatial Manifold: POD basis extraction ($\mathbf{\Phi}$) from static snapshots captures the fundamental "shapes" of deformation. Even in dynamics, the structure will largely move within this low-dimensional spatial subspace.
  • Hyper-Reduction is Mandatory: Static benchmarks showed that Galerkin projection alone is not enough for speed; calculating nonlinear internal forces $\mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{u})$ is the bottleneck. ECSW and DEIM are our "speed kernels".
  • Physical Consistency: ECSW taught us that conserving energy (virtual work) is more robust for nonlinear structures than pure nodal interpolation (DEIM), especially near singularities.

1. The Dynamic Semi-Discrete Equation

In the static phases, we solved for equilibrium: $\mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{u}) = \mathbf{F}_{ext}$. In Phase 4, we introduce Inertia and Damping:

$$\mathbf{M} \ddot{\mathbf{u}} + \mathbf{C} \dot{\mathbf{u}} + \mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{u}) = \mathbf{F}_{ext}(t)$$

Where:

  • $\mathbf{M}$: Global Mass Matrix (consistent or lumped).
  • $\mathbf{C}$: Damping Matrix (usually Rayleigh damping: $\mathbf{C} = \alpha \mathbf{M} + \beta \mathbf{K}$).
  • $\mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{u})$: Nonlinear internal forces (lessons from Phase 3).
  • $\mathbf{F}_{ext}(t)$: Time-dependent external excitation.

2. Time Integration (The Newmark-β Scheme)

To solve the second-order ODE, we discretize time into increments $\Delta t$. We use the Newmark-β method (implicit) for its stability in structural mechanics:

$$\dot{\mathbf{u}}_{n+1} = \dot{\mathbf{u}}_n + [(1-\gamma)\ddot{\mathbf{u}}_n + \gamma \ddot{\mathbf{u}}_{n+1}]\Delta t$$ $$\mathbf{u}_{n+1} = \mathbf{u}_n + \dot{\mathbf{u}}_n \Delta t + [(\frac{1}{2}-\beta)\ddot{\mathbf{u}}_n + \beta \ddot{\mathbf{u}}_{n+1}]\Delta t^2$$

For unconditional stability (Average Acceleration Method), we set $\gamma = 0.5$ and $\beta = 0.25$. This transforms the dynamic problem into a series of "pseudo-static" nonlinear problems at each time step.

3. Projected Dynamics (ROM)

By substituting $\mathbf{u} \approx \mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{q}$ and projecting onto the POD basis, we obtain the reduced dynamic system:

$$\mathbf{M}_r \ddot{\mathbf{q}} + \mathbf{C}_r \dot{\mathbf{q}} + \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{q}) = \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{F}_{ext}(t)$$

Where $\mathbf{M}_r = \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{M} \mathbf{\Phi}$ and $\mathbf{C}_r = \mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{C} \mathbf{\Phi}$ are $k \times k$ matrices (typically $k < 20$).

The Hyper-Reduction Synergy

The term $\mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{\Phi} \mathbf{q})$ is still expensive. In Phase 4, we will apply our ECSW weights $w_e$ from the static training to evaluate this term at a fraction of the cost, enabling **real-time transient structural feedback**.

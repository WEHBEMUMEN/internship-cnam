The Perfect Sphere:
k-Refinement

The peak of Isogeometric Analysis.

1. k-Refinement: The IGA Advantage

In NURBS, the order of refinement matters. If you perform p-refinement (degree elevation) before h-refinement (knot insertion), you achieve a special state called k-refinement.

This results in a basis that is $C^{p-1}$ continuous across the entire mesh. Traditional Finite Elements are typically only $C^0$ between elements. k-refinement creates the smoothest possible mathematical grid.

2. Designing the Exact Sphere in IGA

In Isogeometric Analysis, a sphere is defined as a NURBS surface, allowing for the exact mathematical representation of the CAD geometry. This exactness is achieved by utilizing the rational nature of the basis functions.

Mathematical Formulation

A NURBS surface $\mathbf{S}(u, v)$ is defined as a bivariate tensor product: $$\mathbf{S}(u, v) = \frac{\sum_{i=0}^n \sum_{j=0}^m N_{i,p}(u) M_{j,q}(v) w_{i,j} \mathbf{P}_{i,j}}{\sum_{k=0}^n \sum_{l=0}^m N_{k,p}(u) M_{l,q}(v) w_{k,l}}$$ Where $\mathbf{P}_{i,j}$ represents the grid of control points (the control net) and $w_{i,j}$ are the associated rational weights.

Construction Methods

1. Single-Patch Surface of Revolution

Generated by revolving a semi-circular NURBS curve (degree $p=2$) around a central axis.

  • Pros: Compact representation using a single surface patch.
  • Cons: Creates geometrical singularities ("poles") at the top/bottom point convergences.

2. Multi-Patch "Cubed Sphere"

Avoids poles by projecting the six faces of a cube onto a spherical surface.

  • Pros: Analysis-Suitable Geometry (ASG) with regular clean quadrilaterals.
  • Cons: Requires complex handling of $C^1$ or $C^0$ continuity across interfaces.

Requirements for Exact Representation

  • Rational Weights ($w$): A 90-degree circular arc ($p=2$) physically requires three control points with weights $\{1, \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, 1\}$ to pull the curve into a perfect constant radius circle.
  • Knot Vectors: Open (clamped) knot vectors are generally used, where the first and last knots are repeated $p+1$ times (e.g. $\{0,0,0,1,1,1\}$ for quadratic arcs).
  • Degree ($p$): While degree 2 is the minimum for circular exactness, IGA often uses higher degrees (like cubic $p=3$) to allow for superior inter-element continuity ($C^{p-1}$) during refinement.

By using this NURBS-based definition, the exact CAD geometry of the sphere is preserved throughout the simulation, regardless of how many $h$-, $p$-, or $k$-refinement steps are performed.

Phase 3.4 | Hyper-Reduction

Hyper-Reduction
for Nonlinear ROM

Breaking the Galerkin bottleneck: six methods to achieve true real-time performance.

1. The Galerkin Bottleneck

In a standard Galerkin ROM, each Newton-Raphson iteration requires computing the full internal force $\mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{\Phi q})$ and full tangent stiffness $\mathbf{K}_T(\mathbf{\Phi q})$ by looping over all elements. Only after this expensive assembly is the result projected into the $k$-dimensional reduced space:

The Galerkin Bottleneck

$$\underbrace{\mathbf{\Phi}^T}_{\text{cheap}} \cdot \underbrace{\mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{\Phi q})}_{\text{EXPENSIVE: } \mathcal{O}(N_{el})} \implies \text{Cost } \propto N_{el}, \text{ not } k$$

Hyper-reduction breaks this bottleneck by approximating $\mathbf{F}_{int}$ using only a small subset of elements or DOFs, reducing the assembly cost from $\mathcal{O}(N_{el})$ to $\mathcal{O}(m)$ where $m \ll N_{el}$.

2. Two Families of Approaches

Hyper-reduction methods fall into two main families:

Interpolation-Based

Approximate $\mathbf{F}_{int}$ by evaluating only a subset of DOF entries, then reconstructing the full vector using a nonlinear basis.

Methods: DEIM, UDEIM, Gappy POD

Element Sampling

Select a weighted subset of elements and assemble the projected operators directly from this reduced mesh.

Methods: ECSW, ECM, Collocation (LSPG)

3. DEIM

The Discrete Empirical Interpolation Method (Chaturantabut & Sorensen, 2010) constructs a low-rank approximation of the nonlinear function $\mathbf{F}_{int}(\mathbf{u})$.

Offline: Greedy Index Selection

Apply POD/SVD to snapshots of $\mathbf{F}_{int}$ to obtain a nonlinear basis $\mathbf{U}_f \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times m}$. Then greedily select $m$ interpolation indices $\mathcal{P}$ such that the interpolation problem is well-conditioned:

DEIM Approximation

$$\mathbf{F}_{int} \approx \mathbf{U}_f (\mathbf{P}^T \mathbf{U}_f)^{-1} \mathbf{P}^T \mathbf{F}_{int}$$

Online: Partial Evaluation

Only the $m$ rows of $\mathbf{F}_{int}$ at indices $\mathcal{P}$ need to be computed. The full vector is then reconstructed via the precomputed operator.

4. UDEIM

The Unassembled DEIM (Tiso & Rixen, 2013) applies DEIM to the unassembled element-level force vectors rather than the assembled global vector.

Unassembled Stacking

$$\tilde{\mathbf{f}} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{f}_{e_1} \\ \mathbf{f}_{e_2} \\ \vdots \\ \mathbf{f}_{e_{N_{el}}} \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{N_{el} \cdot N_{dof}}, \quad \mathbf{F}_{int} = \sum_{e=1}^{N_{el}} \mathbf{L}_e^T \mathbf{f}_e$$

DEIM is applied to the stacked vector $\tilde{\mathbf{f}}$, which naturally selects specific elements rather than arbitrary DOFs. This avoids the inconsistency issue of DEIM where selected DOFs may span many elements.

5. Gappy POD

Gappy POD (Everson & Sirovich, 1995) uses the same nonlinear basis $\mathbf{U}_f$ as DEIM but employs oversampled indices and least-squares reconstruction instead of interpolation:

Gappy POD Reconstruction

$$\mathbf{c} = (\mathbf{P}^T \mathbf{U}_f)^{\dagger} \mathbf{P}^T \mathbf{F}_{int}, \quad \mathbf{F}_{int} \approx \mathbf{U}_f \mathbf{c}$$

Using $m_{over} > m$ sampling indices makes the reconstruction overdetermined and more robust to noise, at the cost of slightly more evaluations.

6. ECSW

The Energy-Conserving Sampling and Weighting method (Farhat et al., 2015) directly approximates the projected internal force as a weighted sum over a subset of elements:

ECSW Approximation

$$\mathbf{\Phi}^T \mathbf{F}_{int} \approx \sum_{e \in \mathcal{E}_r} w_e \cdot \mathbf{\Phi}_e^T \mathbf{f}_e, \quad w_e \geq 0$$

The weights $w_e$ are found by solving a Non-Negative Least Squares (NNLS) problem over the training snapshots. Elements with $w_e > 0$ form the reduced mesh. ECSW naturally preserves energy conservation since all weights are non-negative.

7. ECM

The Empirical Cubature Method (Hernández et al., 2017) selects integration points via a greedy column-pivoting strategy on the contribution matrix:

ECM Greedy Selection

$$\text{Select } e^* = \arg\max_{e \notin \mathcal{E}_r} |\mathbf{c}_e^T \mathbf{r}|, \quad \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{G}_{\mathcal{E}_r} \mathbf{w}$$

ECM iteratively adds the element whose contribution has the largest correlation with the current residual, then re-solves for optimal weights. It typically selects fewer elements than ECSW for comparable accuracy.

8. Collocation (LSPG)

The Least-Squares Petrov-Galerkin method (Carlberg et al., 2011) replaces the Galerkin projection with a least-squares minimization of the residual at selected collocation DOFs:

LSPG Formulation

$$\Delta \mathbf{q} = \arg\min_{\delta} \| \mathbf{P}^T \mathbf{R}(\mathbf{\Phi}(\mathbf{q} + \delta)) \|^2$$ $$\implies (\mathbf{J}_P^T \mathbf{J}_P) \Delta\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{J}_P^T \mathbf{R}_P$$

where $\mathbf{J}_P = \mathbf{P}^T \mathbf{K}_T \mathbf{\Phi}$ is the sampled Jacobian. This Petrov-Galerkin formulation provides better stability for convection-dominated problems and non-symmetric systems.
